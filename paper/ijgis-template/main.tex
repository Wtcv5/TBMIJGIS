\begin{abstract}
TBM excavation responses are shaped by changing spatial relations between the
advancing machine and surrounding rock. Monitoring curves and geological voxel
fields, however, do not specify which geological units are plausible
interaction partners for individual TBM components. This study proposes a
geometry-constrained rock--TBM interaction graph computation method for
representing component-indexed anomalous interaction fields. At each excavation
step, TSP-derived geological voxels and component-labelled TBM surface nodes
are organised in a common excavation coordinate, and candidate rock--machine
edges are screened by active-zone, distance, normal-compatibility, and
excavation-state constraints. The resulting sparse candidate-edge set is
aggregated by TBM component to compute a component $\times$ excavation-step
field, including interaction intensity $I_c(t)$ and step-level change
$\Delta I_c(t)$. The field is aligned with monitoring responses and queried
back to contributing rock--machine edges, providing a computable spatial basis
for excavation anomaly readout. Two tunnel cases show that the graph
computation generates component-level anomaly fields that can be compared with
monitoring responses; the clearer SJLS interval exhibits stronger
residual-consistent co-variation than the BSLL intervals. The proposed
computation is therefore treated as a queryable summary of screened candidate
relations, not as measured contact force, calibrated jamming probability, or a
universal operational risk rule.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Data availability statement}
The raw TBM monitoring and TSP data are project data and are not publicly
available because they contain engineering and site information. The materials
that can be shared are the experiment configurations, analysis scripts, figure
scripts, and derived result summaries used to reproduce the reported tables and
figures. These materials are available from the corresponding author upon
reasonable request, subject to project data-use permissions; the raw site data
are not redistributed. To support reproducibility of the spatial graph
construction without disclosing site data, the authors can also provide a
synthetic demonstration package containing a small voxel field, TBM surface
nodes, candidate edge lists, field-calculation scripts, and residual
diagnostic scripts.

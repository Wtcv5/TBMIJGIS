\begin{abstract}
Existing representations of rock--TBM interaction during excavation treat monitoring
responses as temporal sequences, which preserve temporal dependence but lack the
spatial explicitness needed to resolve where interactions occur, which machine
components are implicated, and how interaction structure evolves along chainage.
This study proposes a response-supervised, geometry-constrained graph sequence
framework for spatially explicit representation and interpretable learning of
rock--TBM interaction.  The framework introduces three key innovations:
(i)~a heterogeneous graph-sequence representation that formalizes TSP-derived
rock voxels and parameterized TBM surface nodes as spatial entities with explicit
candidate interaction relations; (ii)~a geometry-constrained edge construction
strategy based on spatial proximity, signed-normal consistency, active-zone
filtering, and excavation-state constraints, which restricts learning to spatially
plausible rock--machine relations rather than unconstrained connectivity; and
(iii)~a diagnostic relevance aggregation procedure that summarises raw
rock--machine edge scores at surface-node, component, and chainage levels to
support response-consistent spatial diagnostics.  Rather than relying on sparse jamming labels or
manually calibrated contact forces, the framework uses continuously recorded
monitoring responses to supervise the relative importance of geometry-screened
candidate relations, enabling interpretable spatial learning under incomplete
observability.  The evaluation uses two tunnel cases: BSLL DyK1017+205 and
SJLS Dyk1252+411.  BSLL is used for one-step, multi-step, and interpretation
analysis, while SJLS provides an external TSP case for testing prediction
consistency and geometry ablation.  The framework remains competitive with
temporal baselines, and SJLS is consistent with a small case-specific
geometry-related MAE difference,
but the experiments do not establish a broad forecasting improvement.  The
more consistent evidence is interpretive:
checkpoint-based diagnostics show positively clustered diagnostic relevance in all
evaluated runs, relevance that is not simply explained by candidate-edge
degree, and case-specific association with local TSP velocity and monitoring
responses.  These patterns are reported as response-consistent diagnostics, not
as proof of actual contact conditions or geological causality.  By unifying geological
sensing, machine geometry, monitoring response, and diagnostic relevance
within a constrained graph sequence, the framework offers a spatially explicit
approach to excavation interaction analysis that complements existing temporal
prediction methods.
\end{abstract}

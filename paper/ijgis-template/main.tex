\begin{abstract}
Mechanized tunnelling produces a moving spatial configuration in which geological anomalies do not affect the tunnel boring machine (TBM) as an undifferentiated object, but through their changing spatial relations with the cutterhead and shield components. Existing TBM monitoring records describe machine responses such as shield pressure, shield displacement, thrust and advance rate, while tunnel seismic prediction (TSP) data describe where weak or anomalous ground may occur. However, these two data sources do not directly represent which geological units are spatially related to which TBM components as excavation advances. This study proposes a dynamic rock--TBM spatial relation graph to represent candidate spatial relations between TSP-derived rock voxels and component-labelled TBM surface nodes. Candidate rock--TBM edges are generated under active-zone, distance, normal-compatibility and excavation-state constraints, and their spatial relation weights are used to compute a component-level geological anomaly index. The method is evaluated through an event-centred spatial interpretation of a real shield-sticking event in the Boshulaling Tunnel. The event was characterized by increasing shield pressure, converging shield displacement, increasing total thrust and nearly zero advance rate while the cutterhead continued rotating. The results show that the graph converts TSP-derived geological anomalies into component-level anomaly indices, reconstructs the dynamic association between low-velocity rock voxels and shield components, and supports edge-level provenance from the event interpretation back to specific rock voxels, TBM surface nodes and candidate rock--TBM edges. The proposed graph is therefore a spatial data representation for component-level interpretation of TBM excavation anomalies, rather than a contact-force estimator, calibrated risk model or general failure-prediction model.

\end{abstract}

\section*{Data availability statement}
The raw TBM monitoring and TSP data are project data and are not publicly
available because they contain engineering and site information. The materials
that can be shared are the experiment configurations, analysis scripts, figure
scripts, and derived result summaries used to reproduce the reported tables and
figures. These materials are available from the corresponding author upon
reasonable request, subject to project data-use permissions; the raw site data
are not redistributed. To support reproducibility of the spatial graph construction without disclosing site data, the authors can also provide a synthetic demonstration package containing a small voxel field, TBM surface nodes, candidate edge lists, component-level geological anomaly index scripts, and edge-level provenance examples.

TBM excavation is a moving spatial interaction process, not a fixed-site
monitoring problem. As the cutterhead and shield advance, geological units
enter, approach, and leave the machine's potential interaction zone. Changes
in thrust, torque, penetration, advance rate, and shield pressure therefore
reflect both operational control and the evolving spatial configuration between
geological anomalies and machine components.

Monitoring-based TBM response models are useful because short-horizon
excavation responses often have strong temporal persistence. Tabular and
sequence-based models learn this persistence from recent operational and
response histories
\citep{yagiz2008utilizing,hassanpour2010tbm,armaghani2018prediction,hochreiter1997long}.
However, a monitoring curve does not state which geological unit is adjacent to
the cutterhead or shield, whether the adjacency is geometrically plausible, or
how the candidate rock--machine relation set changes as excavation proceeds.
This limits the spatial interpretability of monitoring-only analyses.

Advance geological prediction and TSP surveys provide the complementary view:
they describe spatial variation in the ground ahead of or around excavation
\citep{li2019tunnel}. These data can be converted into chainage-referenced
geological voxels or attribute fields, preserving where low-velocity or other
geological anomalies occur. Yet a geological voxel field by itself answers
where an anomaly is located, not which TBM component is a plausible interaction
partner at a particular excavation step. A component-indexed field
representation must therefore link geological spatial support to machine
surface geometry and excavation state.

To address this gap, the paper treats rock--TBM interpretation as a spatial
entity--relation modelling problem. The input consists of a TSP-derived rock
voxel field, an advancing parameterized TBM surface, and
excavation-step-aligned monitoring records. Two graph entity types are defined:
rock voxels and TBM surface nodes. Monitoring responses are aligned to
excavation steps but are not graph entities. The method then constructs a
geometry-constrained sparse rock--TBM interaction graph at each excavation step
using active-zone, distance, normal-compatibility, and excavation-state
constraints. Weighted rock--machine edges are aggregated by TBM component to
compute a component $\times$ excavation-step anomalous interaction field. The
field contains $I_c(t)$ and $\Delta I_c(t)$, can be aligned with monitoring
responses, and remains traceable back to individual rock--machine edges. The
graph is introduced first as a spatial data model and graph computation
method, not as a predictive graph-learning architecture.

The contribution lies in integrating these elements into one
excavation-step-indexed spatial graph computation, rather than in claiming a
new high-accuracy predictor. The paper contributes: (i) a rock--TBM spatial entity model that
places TSP-derived rock voxels and TBM surface components in a common
excavation coordinate while keeping monitoring responses as aligned records;
(ii) a per-step geometry-constrained sparse rock--machine interaction graph
built from active-zone membership, distance decay, surface-normal
compatibility, and excavation-state constraints; and (iii) a component
$\times$ excavation-step anomalous interaction field that aggregates
fixed-reference geological anomaly scores over screened candidate relations per
TBM component and tracks $I_c(t)$ and $\Delta I_c(t)$ over excavation steps.
The empirical evidence is used to support representation, traceability, and
selected-interval residual consistency, not operational fault attribution or
forecasting superiority.

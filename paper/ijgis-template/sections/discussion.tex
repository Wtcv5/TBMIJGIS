\subsection{Spatial representation contribution}
\label{sec:graph_necessity}
The main contribution is an excavation-step-indexed spatial relation model,
not a higher-accuracy response predictor. The model constructs a component
$\times$ excavation-step anomalous interaction field from a TSP-derived rock
voxel field and an advancing parameterized TBM surface, then aligns this field
with monitoring responses. Monitoring sequences describe response inertia, and
TSP voxels describe geological heterogeneity, but neither representation alone states
which geological voxels are candidate interaction partners for which TBM
components at a given chainage. The proposed graph closes this representational
gap by linking rock voxels, TBM surface nodes, and geometry-compatible
candidate edges in one excavation coordinate system, and by aligning the
resulting field with monitoring residuals to support
excavation anomaly discovery and jamming-risk-oriented interpretation.

For spatial modelling, the graph is an explicit data representation rather
than a predictive architecture. It has defined entities, relation types,
screening rules, edge weights, component aggregation, and traceability back to
individual candidate relations. The field results show that this representation
organises rock--machine relations into component-indexed field readouts.

From a digital-earth perspective, the contribution is an underground spatial
data modelling layer for an advancing construction process. It organises static
geological support and advancing TBM surface geometry into
excavation-step-indexed spatial relations that can be inspected and queried,
while monitoring records provide aligned response readouts. The layer is
compatible with digital twin workflows, but it addresses a narrower
representational problem: how to make the rock--machine relation support
explicit before any prediction, control, or risk-calibration module is built.

\subsection{Diagnostic readout beyond monitoring curves}
\label{sec:descriptor_value}
Comparing $I_c(t)$ with persistence residuals reframes the evaluation question
from ``does the graph beat persistence?'' to ``does the graph encode spatial
diagnostic information consistent with the response changes left after
persistence?'' This framing matches the present data scale.
The SJLS results illustrate the value of this framing: raw chainage and global
Vp summaries are substantially associated with the selected residual, but their
associations largely disappear after linear detrending, whereas the proposed
candidate-edge field readout remains strong.
The step-change readout further shows a large negative association between
$\Delta I_c(t)$ and the step-level shield-pressure residual change in SJLS,
supporting the interpretation of the output as an excavation-step anomaly
field rather than only a static geological summary.

The BSLL results show that the graph and field readouts are constructed
consistently in compact intervals, while residual-consistency evidence is more
sensitive to detrending and anomaly definition. This contrast defines the
claim boundary: the evidence supports a spatial field-representation
claim, not a universal component--response law.

\subsection{Evidence scope across tunnel cases}
\label{sec:evidential_strength}
The contrast between SJLS and BSLL is not treated as a success--failure
dichotomy. It indicates that field-readout usefulness depends on geological contrast,
monitoring quality, operational stability, and the length of the evaluated
chainage interval. SJLS contains a longer test interval and a clearer external
TSP velocity contrast, which makes field--residual co-variation more
visible. The BSLL intervals are compact and may be more strongly affected by
operational control and local construction variability.

The circular-shift checks are also read conservatively. They are useful
as interval-level diagnostics for monotonic sequence structure, while formal
multiplicity-adjusted testing would require broader evidence. The most coherent
support comes from the
combined pattern: fixed diagnostic pairs, detrended comparison, field-readout
null models, threshold sensitivity, anomaly-definition sensitivity, and
edge-level traceability. The relation-support ablation adds a more specific
boundary: component pooling weakens the fixed diagnostic associations, whereas
removing the normal-compatibility threshold or distance--normal weighting has
little effect in the current samples. The supported claim is therefore centred
on component-indexed candidate-edge aggregation rather than a separate
performance gain from geometry weighting alone.

The step-wise component-label disruption diagnostic
(Table~\ref{tab:component_permutation}) is retained only as a label-disruption
check. Because it shuffles labels independently at each chainage step, it does
not preserve complete component trajectories and should not be read as proof
that physically different TBM components are exchangeable. The whole-series
component reassignment diagnostic (Table~\ref{tab:component_series_reassignment})
is more conservative and shows that the fixed target component is not always
the strongest complete component series. The evidence therefore supports
candidate-edge field consistency more strongly than unique
component-label specificity.

\subsection{Interpretation boundaries}
\label{sec:interpretation_boundary}
The proposed field readouts are summaries of screened TSP-derived anomalies around
TBM components, with geometry weights retained for edge-level traceability.
They are not measured contact force, contact pressure, jamming probability,
causal attribution, or calibrated operational risk. This boundary follows from
the graph definition: it encodes candidate spatial relations, not observed
physical contact. Independent engineering labels would be required before the
field readouts could be interpreted as risk probabilities or contact-force
estimates.
The edge-contribution concentration results also show that the field readout is
distributed across many screened candidate relations. Edge-level query should
therefore be interpreted as ranked spatial traceability, not as identification
of a small set of physically causal contact edges.

A second boundary concerns the standardized graph support. All three formal
runs use the same parameterized TBM surface, active-zone geometry, surface
sampling resolution, and edge thresholds, so the per-step node and edge counts
are the same across cases by construction. This standardization keeps the
field readout comparable across cases and prevents graph-size variation from being
confounded with geological contrast, but it also means that the current
framework cannot test whether a different TBM geometry, a coarser surface
sampling, or a different active-zone extent would change the diagnostic
pattern. The standardized support is therefore both a comparability device and
a scope limitation: the reported evidence is conditional on one graph template,
and sensitivity to machine geometry and active-zone dimensions remains for
future work.

\subsection{Limitations and future work}
\label{sec:limitations}
Several limitations remain. First, the evidence is interval-specific: it comes
from two tunnel cases and their evaluated intervals. The reported associations
are therefore descriptive diagnostics rather than case-general
component--response laws. Second,
TSP inversion uncertainty is not propagated through the voxel graph; future
work should attach uncertainty to voxel attributes and evaluate how inversion
and spatial resolution affect candidate relation sets. For the SJLS case in
particular, the geological support is a 1D-extruded velocity profile rather
than a fully 3D-inverted voxel field, so cross-sectional geological variation
is not captured by the current field readout.

Third, the step-wise component-label disruption diagnostic only disrupts labels within
the standardized graph support. Larger datasets would allow stronger null
designs and inference that accounts for multiple comparisons. Fourth, the
study lacks independent engineering labels such as jamming records, shield
pressure anomaly logs, construction intervention logs, or geological exposure
records. Such evidence would be required before the field readouts could be
evaluated as operational risk indicators. Finally, the main field readout uses a
transparent Vp-based low-velocity anomaly score. Multi-attribute scores using
Vs, modulus, Vp/Vs, Poisson's ratio, or density require explicit weighting
rules or independent validation before they can replace the main field readout.

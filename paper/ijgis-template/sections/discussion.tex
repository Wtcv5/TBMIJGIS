\subsection{What the graph representation adds beyond monitoring-only prediction}
\label{sec:tradeoff}
The revised experiments sharpen the paper's central claim. The framework should
not be presented as a broad forecasting model. In BSLL, the one-step
and three-step runs are essentially tied with Persistence and do not show a
consistent benefit from constrained topology. In SJLS, the full model gives a small MAE
decrease relative to Persistence, randomised edges, and the no-prior variant, but the
difference is modest. This pattern is meaningful because it separates prediction
accuracy from representation: the principal value is not forecasting error
reduction, but the conversion of a monitoring sequence into an inspectable
spatial entity-relation process.

This distinction changes the interpretation of the contribution. The prediction
task is used as response supervision and as a consistency check. It is not the
only, or even the most consistent, evidence for the method. The more consistent evidence is
that TSP voxels, TBM surface components, candidate rock--machine relations,
monitoring responses, and diagnostic relevance summaries remain connected in a
single spatial representation. The paper's value therefore lies in making
rock--TBM interaction inspectable as a spatial process under incomplete
observability. Monitoring-only baselines can predict response variables, but they
cannot organise TSP voxels, TBM surface components, and screened candidate
rock--machine relations into the same spatial diagnostic structure.

BSLL and SJLS should be read as complementary evidence rather than as a
development-validation pair. BSLL provides a compact case in which the limits of
prediction are visible: the model is competitive but does not improve on simple
temporal persistence, and randomised edges are not worse. This is useful because
it prevents the paper from overclaiming geometry as an accuracy mechanism. At
the same time, BSLL provides interpretable multi-step and component-chainage
examples that illustrate what a graph-sequence representation adds beyond
scalar prediction.

SJLS plays a different role. It uses an external TSP-derived velocity field and
a larger number of forward test samples. In this case, the full geometry-aware
model has slightly lower MAE than the ablated variants, and the learned relevance
has a high absolute association with the local TSP field. This makes SJLS the clearer
case for arguing that geometry-constrained representation can support
response-consistent prediction and spatial interpretation. The important point
is not that SJLS proves a large prediction improvement, but that it shows the same
representation operating in a second tunnel setting with a clearer
geometry-ablation pattern.

\subsection{Why prediction gains are limited}
The prediction results do not show a broad accuracy improvement, and several
factors explain why this is expected rather than surprising. First, the test
sets are small, especially for BSLL, which limits the statistical power of any
accuracy comparison. Second, the mileage-ordered split is a forward
extrapolation setting: validation and testing are performed on later continuous
tunnel intervals that may exhibit geological distribution shift, so Persistence
is a strong baseline that benefits from local autocorrelation. Third, TBM
monitoring responses are themselves strongly time-autocorrelated, which makes
short-horizon forecasting difficult to beat with any added spatial structure.
Fourth, TSP-derived geological attributes and TBM monitoring responses operate
at different spatial and temporal scales, and the current alignment may not
fully exploit the geological information. These factors together explain why
the full graph-sequence model remains comparable to Persistence in BSLL and
shows only a small geometry-related difference in SJLS.

This limited prediction gain does not negate the contribution. The graph
representation provides spatially organised rock--machine relation diagnostics
that temporal baselines cannot produce. Moran's $I$ is positive in all three
runs, and the degree-control correlations are negative, supporting a bounded
but useful claim: the diagnostic relevance views are not spatially random and are
not simply inflated by the number of candidate edges incident to a TBM node.
The experiments motivate a GIScience framing of TBM excavation analysis: the
problem is not merely to add more features to a forecasting model, but to
formalise heterogeneous spatial entities with different supports and aggregate
model-derived relevance into spatial views that support inspection, comparison,
and case-based reasoning.

\subsection{Interpretation boundary and future validation}
Several limitations remain. The test sets are small, especially for BSLL, and
all results are based on single fixed-seed formal runs. Negative $R^2$ values
show that forward extrapolation remains difficult under geological shift. The
observed attention--geology correlations may partly reflect chainage trends and
therefore require spatial detrending before broader geological interpretation
is made.

Future work should prioritise multi-seed stability, label-shuffle controls,
candidate-edge threshold sensitivity, spatial detrending, and additional tunnel
sections. Independent event labels, expert assessments, contact measurements, or
shield-pressure distribution observations would be required before the relevance maps could be connected to
operational risk or actual contact conditions. Until then, the defensible claim
is representation and response-consistent interpretation, not contact-condition recovery
or general predictive dominance.

\subsection{What the current experiments actually support}
\label{sec:tradeoff}
The revised experiments sharpen the paper's central claim. The framework should
not be presented as a broadly superior forecasting model. In BSLL, the one-step
and three-step runs are essentially tied with Persistence and do not benefit
clearly from constrained topology. In SJLS, the full model gives a small MAE
advantage over Persistence, randomised edges, and the no-prior variant, but the
gain is modest. This pattern is meaningful because it separates accuracy from
representation: the model is not winning by a large margin, but it provides a
spatial entity-relation structure that temporal baselines cannot produce.

This distinction changes the interpretation of the contribution. The prediction
task is used as response supervision and as a consistency check. It is not the
only, or even the strongest, evidence for the method. The stronger evidence is
that TSP voxels, TBM surface components, candidate rock--machine relations,
monitoring responses, and projected relevance diagnostics remain connected in a
single spatial representation. The paper's value therefore lies in making
rock--TBM interaction inspectable as a spatial process under incomplete
observability.

\subsection{Why the two tunnel cases are complementary}
BSLL and SJLS should be read as complementary evidence rather than as a
development-validation pair. BSLL provides a compact case in which the limits of
prediction are visible: the model is competitive but does not outperform simple
temporal persistence, and randomised edges are not worse. This is useful because
it prevents the paper from overclaiming geometry as an accuracy mechanism. At
the same time, BSLL provides interpretable multi-step and surface-evolution
examples that illustrate what a graph-sequence representation adds beyond
scalar prediction.

SJLS plays a different role. It uses an external TSP-derived velocity field and
a larger number of forward test samples. In this case, the full geometry-aware
model is slightly better than the ablated variants, and the learned relevance
is strongly associated with the local TSP field. This makes SJLS the better
case for arguing that geometry-constrained representation can support
response-consistent prediction and spatial interpretation. The important point
is not that SJLS proves a large accuracy gain, but that it shows the same
representation operating in a second tunnel setting with a clearer
geometry-sensitive signal.

\subsection{Spatial interpretation as the main added value}
The most consistent result across the experiments is spatial organisation of
the learned surface relevance. Moran's $I$ is positive in all three runs, and
the degree-control correlations are negative. These two facts support a bounded
but useful claim: the surface relevance maps are not spatially random and are
not simply inflated by the number of candidate edges incident to a TBM node.

This does not make the maps physical contact-force maps. The relevance values
are produced by a response-supervised neural model and remain conditional on
the forecasting objective, graph construction rules, and training data. Their
proper role is diagnostic: they indicate where the model places response-linked
importance on the TBM surface under geometry-screened rock--machine candidate
relations. This diagnostic role is still valuable because conventional
monitoring-only sequence models cannot localise response relevance on a TBM
surface or compare cutterhead and shield regions.

\subsection{Implications for GIScience representation}
The experiments motivate a GIScience framing of TBM excavation analysis. The
problem is not merely to add more features to a forecasting model. It is to
formalise heterogeneous spatial entities with different supports: TSP voxels,
TBM surface nodes, rock--machine candidate relations, and chainage-indexed
monitoring responses. Once these entities are represented explicitly, model
outputs can be projected back into spatial views that support inspection,
comparison, and case-based reasoning.

This representation perspective also explains why prediction and spatial
diagnosis must be evaluated separately. A temporal baseline may be difficult to
beat on short, autocorrelated monitoring series, especially under a
mileage-ordered split. However, such a baseline cannot answer where the model
places interaction relevance, whether relevance is spatially clustered, or how
the relevance pattern differs between BSLL and SJLS. The graph-sequence
framework contributes this second evaluation axis.

\subsection{Limitations and next steps}
Several limitations remain. The test sets are small, especially for BSLL, and
all results are based on single fixed-seed formal runs. Negative $R^2$ values
show that forward extrapolation remains difficult under geological shift. The
observed attention--geology correlations may partly reflect chainage trends and
therefore require spatial detrending before stronger geological interpretation
is made.

Future work should prioritise multi-seed stability, label-shuffle controls,
candidate-edge threshold sensitivity, spatial detrending, and additional tunnel
sections. Independent event labels, expert assessments, or physical
measurements would be required before the relevance maps could be connected to
operational risk or actual contact conditions. Until then, the defensible claim
is representation and response-consistent interpretation, not physical recovery
or general predictive superiority.

\subsection{Scope and interpretive boundaries}
The experiments evaluate a spatial representation rather than a universally
superior forecasting model. Three case runs are used: BSLL DyK1017+205 with
one-step prediction, BSLL DyK1017+205 with a three-step prediction horizon, and
SJLS Dyk1252+411 with an external TSP-derived velocity field. The learned edge
and surface relevance values are response-consistent model diagnostics; they
are not direct measurements of contact force, contact pressure, jamming
probability, or engineering risk.

\subsection{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}
All formal runs use a mileage-ordered split so that testing is performed on a
later continuous tunnel interval. The BSLL one-step run contains 30 training
samples and 8 test samples (target chainages 41--48\,m). The BSLL three-step
run contains 29 training samples and 7 test samples (target chainages
42--48\,m). The SJLS run contains 76 training samples and 17 test samples
(target chainages 99--115\,m). The graph construction uses
$\tau_{\mathrm{edge}}=2.0$\,m, $\eta_{\min}=0.3$, and
$\tau_{\mathrm{zone}}=5.0$\,m. The temporal history window is $K=5$.

\subsection{Forward prediction across the two tunnel cases}
Table~\ref{tab:case_prediction} reports the case-level forward prediction
metrics. The results support a cautious interpretation. In BSLL, the one-step
graph-sequence model obtains MAE 0.415, essentially matching Persistence
(0.415) and the random-edge variant (0.415). The three-step BSLL run similarly
remains close to Persistence (0.462 versus 0.462), while the random-edge
variant is slightly lower in MAE (0.461). These BSLL results do not support a
claim of predictive superiority.

The SJLS case gives a more favourable but still small effect. The full model
obtains MAE 0.2553, slightly lower than Persistence (0.2554), randomised edges
(0.2559), and the no-prior variant (0.2570). The Pearson correlation is high
($r=0.960$), but $R^2$ remains negative because the mileage-ordered test
interval is a demanding extrapolation setting. Thus the SJLS result is best
read as a small response-consistency advantage under the external TSP case, not
as a large accuracy breakthrough.

\subsection{Graph ablations and the role of geometry}
The ablations separate two questions that should not be conflated: whether the
graph-sequence representation improves prediction, and whether
geometry-constrained relations produce interpretable spatial diagnostics.
Prediction evidence for the first question is mixed. In SJLS, removing the
geometric prior increases MAE from 0.2553 to 0.2570, and randomising
rock--machine edges increases MAE to 0.2559. This supports a small
geometry-sensitive effect in the external TSP case. In BSLL, however,
randomised edges are comparable to or slightly better than the full model. The
BSLL ablation therefore should not be used to claim that constrained topology
improves forecasting accuracy.

\subsection{Spatial consistency of learned surface relevance}
\label{sec:spatial_consistency}
The interpretation evidence is more consistent across cases.
Table~\ref{tab:spatial_consistency} reports four post-hoc indicators computed
from the saved checkpoints. Surface relevance is positively spatially
autocorrelated in all three runs: Moran's $I=0.546$ for BSLL h=1, 0.302 for
BSLL h=3, and 0.300 for SJLS. This indicates spatially organised relevance
fields rather than random surface noise. The degree-control correlations are
negative in all cases ($-0.229$, $-0.324$, and $-0.456$), which argues against
a trivial explanation in which nodes appear important only because they have
more incident candidate edges.

The geology association differs by case. BSLL shows a strong negative
association between mean surface relevance and local TSP velocity ($r=-0.989$
for h=1 and $r=-0.688$ for h=3), whereas SJLS shows a strong positive
association ($r=0.870$). The signs should not be interpreted as universal
physical rules; they indicate that learned relevance co-varies with the local
TSP field within each tunnel setting. The strongest response-linked
association also differs: AdvanceRate for BSLL and Torque for SJLS. This
case-specific behaviour shows that the graph-to-surface route can expose
different response-relevance patterns rather than producing a single generic
hotspot template.

\subsection{Robustness and remaining checks}
\label{sec:robustness}
The current evidence supports three bounded claims: the framework remains
competitive with temporal baselines, SJLS shows a small geometry-sensitive
accuracy effect, and all three saved checkpoints produce spatially organised
surface relevance. It does not support claims of statistically significant
global accuracy improvement, measured physical interaction recovery, or
operational risk calibration. Multi-seed stability, label permutation,
threshold sensitivity, spatial detrending, and additional cross-project
validation remain necessary before stronger robustness claims can be made.

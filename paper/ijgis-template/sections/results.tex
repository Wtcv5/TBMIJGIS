\subsection{Forward prediction check across case roles}
\label{sec:prediction_ablation}
Table~\ref{tab:case_prediction} reports the case-level forward prediction
metrics. The results support a cautious interpretation. In BSLL, the one-step
graph-sequence model obtains MAE 0.4147, essentially matching Persistence
(0.4146) and the random-edge variant (0.4145). The three-step BSLL run
similarly remains close to Persistence (0.4617 versus 0.4618), while the
random-edge variant is slightly lower in MAE (0.4609). These BSLL results do
not support a claim that the graph sequence improves prediction in this case.

The SJLS case gives a more favourable but still small effect. The full model
obtains MAE 0.2553, slightly lower than Persistence (0.2554), randomised edges
(0.2559), and the no-prior variant (0.2570). The Pearson correlation is high
($r=0.960$), but $R^2$ remains negative because the mileage-ordered test
interval is a demanding extrapolation setting. Thus the SJLS result is best
read as a small response-consistency difference under the external TSP case, not
as a large prediction breakthrough.

\subsection{Geometry ablation and response consistency}
\label{sec:geometry_ablation}

\emph{Graph ablations and the role of geometry.}
The ablations separate two questions that should not be conflated: whether the
graph-sequence representation improves prediction, and whether
geometry-constrained relations produce interpretable spatial diagnostics.
Prediction evidence for the first question is mixed. In SJLS, removing the
geometric prior increases MAE from 0.2553 to 0.2570, and randomising
rock--machine edges increases MAE to 0.2559. This is consistent with a small
case-specific geometry-related difference in the external TSP case. In BSLL, however,
randomised edges are comparable to or slightly better than the full model. The
BSLL ablation therefore should not be used to claim that constrained topology
improves forecasting accuracy.

\subsection{Spatial organisation of learned diagnostic relevance}
\label{sec:spatial_consistency}
Because the prediction differences are small, the following sections focus on
whether the graph representation produces spatially organised diagnostic
relevance.

The interpretation evidence is more consistent across cases.
Table~\ref{tab:spatial_consistency} reports four post-hoc indicators computed
from the saved checkpoints. Diagnostic relevance is positively spatially
autocorrelated in all three runs: Moran's $I=0.546$ for BSLL h=1, 0.302 for
BSLL h=3, and 0.300 for SJLS. This indicates spatially organised relevance
fields rather than random surface noise. The degree-control correlations are
negative in all cases ($-0.229$, $-0.324$, and $-0.456$), which argues against
a trivial explanation in which nodes appear important only because they have
more incident candidate edges.

The geology association differs by case. BSLL shows a high absolute correlation
between mean diagnostic relevance and local TSP velocity ($r=-0.989$ for h=1
and $r=-0.688$ for h=3), whereas SJLS shows a high positive correlation
($r=0.870$). The signs should not be interpreted as universal physical rules;
they indicate that learned relevance co-varies with the local TSP field within
each tunnel setting. The response variable with the highest absolute association
also differs: AdvanceRate for BSLL and Torque for SJLS. This case-specific
behaviour shows that the diagnostic aggregation route can expose different
response-relevance patterns rather than producing a single generic template.

\subsection{Robustness and remaining checks}
\label{sec:robustness}
The current evidence supports three bounded claims: the framework remains
competitive with temporal baselines, SJLS is consistent with a small
case-specific geometry-related accuracy difference, and all three saved
checkpoints produce spatially organised diagnostic relevance. It does not support
claims of broad global accuracy improvement, measured contact-condition recovery, or
operational risk calibration. As a minimal sanity check, paired permutation
tests using the saved predictions show that the full model is not separable
from Persistence in MAE for BSLL h=1 ($p=0.3948$), BSLL h=3 ($p=0.1659$), or
SJLS h=1 ($p=0.3198$), although SJLS has lower MAE than the weaker learned
baselines in this run. Multi-seed stability, label-shuffle retraining,
threshold sensitivity, spatial detrending, and additional cross-project
validation remain necessary before broader robustness claims can be made.

1. Introduction
In the last decade, spatial-social networking emerged as the integration of location-based
services and social networks to deliver valuable and personalized services to users.
Spatial-social networks, also known as location-based social networks (LBSN), generate
an increasing amount of user location data available for a variety of services, such as
location (Zheng and Xie 2011), activity and friend recommendations (Zheng 2011), social
wayfinding (Karimi 2011), and many private or business applications (Krishnamurthy and
Pelechrinis 2013, Lee and Ye 2014, Bao et al. 2015, Huiji and Huan 2015). In contrast to
outdoor contexts where an extensive range of applications and modeling approaches has
been developed for years, this is not specifically the case to date for indoor spaces that still
need additional research developments in close relation with social behaviors (Jenkins
et al. 1992, Klepeis et al. 2001, Brasche and Bischof 2005).
    The number of spatial-social potential applications is relatively large in indoor spaces,
from location recommendation and event discovery to friendship interactions. Most
indoor occupants have limited knowledge of unfrequented indoor places despite their
eagerness to know more about their indoor surroundings through location recommenda­
tion services. Indoor areas might promote a sense of community belonging among
people, as they are more likely to generate a few acquaintances through recommendation

services. Relatively shorter distances and constrained movements within buildings favor
face-to-face interactions among indoor occupants, thus promoting serendipitous social
interactions. These features also allow people to act on calls for events’ contribution
quickly (Shema and Huang 2016).
    Indoor spatial-social services designed and considered as integrated systems should be
useful for an extensive range of applications. As a practical instance, a shopping mall
manager can use an LBSN’s check-in data to store recommendations, understand shop­
ping habits, and even optimize a shopping mall’s spatial configuration (Omer and
Goldblatt 2017, Ren et al. 2017, Zhang and Zhu 2017). Besides, an office management
system can use an indoor LBSN’s check-in data to track office employees’ locations and
sense their interactions. Analyzing these data provides valuable insights to optimize the
office’s spatial layout, to connect colleagues through expected formal interactions or
unexpected informal encounters, and to increase inter-group and intra-group innovation
and productivity (Wang et al. 2010, Zhu et al. 2010, Brown 2014).
    Indoor environments usually have specific spatial, organizational, and functional proper­
ties, different from outdoor areas, that impose unique properties on an indoor spatial-social
network’s data model. Indoor environments are larger-scale clustered areas and are more
structured than outdoor environments (Richter et al. 2011). Furthermore, indoor collocations
have relatively shorter distances (Shema and Huang 2016). So, the concepts of places and
their topological relations are more compatible with the human spatio-cognitive perception
of indoor spaces rather than pinpoint positions and physical distances (Si and Arikawa 2015,
Sithole and Zlatanova 2016). As well, a building’s structural layout, organizational subdivision,
and subspaces’ functions directly affect formal and informal face-to-face interactions of LBSN’s
users and influence knowledge sharing and innovative cooperation between them (Wineman
et al. 2009, Sailer and McCulloh 2012, Brown 2014, Appel-Meulenbroek et al. 2017, Khazanchi
et al. 2018, Small and Adler 2019). An indoor LBSN data model should consider the indoor
environment’s spatial and architectural structure, the topological relations of connected
indoor places, and its organizational and functional subspaces.
    Graphs have already proven themselves as valuable data structures to model spatial-
social data (Saleem 2018). They also have been used to represent locations, relations, and
activity history of humans acting in an indoor space (Winter et al. 2017, 2019, Hua et al.
2019, Guo et al. 2020). Graphs and networks offer a powerful language to describe and
study pairwise interaction between people and places (Fan 2017, Wang et al. 2020).
Practically, LBSN’s data are usually non-uniform while intrinsically interconnected. As
graph structures are essential parts of most spatial-social queries, these features make
graph databases and their query languages more efficient than relational databases in
storing and querying spatial-social data (Angles et al. 2013).
    Despite the analytical power of graphs and networks, they have a significant limitation as
they can only represent binary relations between pairs of things. Human–place interactions
are not limited to binary relations, as humans can interact with a given place in different
explicit and implicit ways. N-ary relations provide a promising approach to explore various
types of relevant information carried by the underlying network (Tarakci 2017, Wang et al.
2020). Simplices can represent n-ary relations with n vertices as natural generalizations of the
vertices and directed edges of a network (Johnson 2013). Q-analysis of simplicial complexes
provides a rational way for describing topological aspects of higher-order n-ary relations
between users and places, as ‘traces of n-ary relations’ (Legrand 2002, Qi et al. 2015).

    This research develops a graph-based data model specifically oriented to indoor places
and LBSN users. The model is enriched by supporting traces of n-ary relations among
users and places using Q-analysis of simplicial complexes. By adopting simplex-based
similarity/relevance measures, the proposed model considers place-place, user-user, and
user–place relations. These relations indicate how people interact with some given places
while moving in an indoor environment and evaluate the degree of interactions between
users and places. This research’s indoor space model also incorporates a simplex-based
metric between pairs of indoor places, representing the ease of access between indoor
places. These properties are particularly relevant when processing indoor spatial-social
queries.
    This paper’s remainder is structured as follows: Section 2 reviews related works on
indoor spatial-social networking. Section 3 briefly introduces the theoretical foundations
of the representation methodology. Section 4 describes the main principles behind the
graph-based modeling of users and places and the model’s simplex-based enrichment.
Section 5 discusses the experimental prototype’s qualitative capability and quantitative
efficiency in processing common spatial-social queries in an indoor environment. Finally,
the last section concludes the paper and outlines further work.


2. Related works on indoor spatial-social networking
Spatial-social networks are online social networks that log users’ location information at
different places and times. This information can be logical, symbolic, semantic, and
associated with contextual information about the areas where users act. Voluntarily
shared check-ins to discrete locations and times can make up a spatial-social network.
Information extracted from analyzing these spatial-social networks’ data can improve
traditional social networks’ existing applications and act as data sources for additional
services. These services usually study user mobility behavior for many applications such as
product promotion, route planning, and recommendation services (Zheng 2011, Zheng
and Xie 2011, Krishnamurthy and Pelechrinis 2013, Anwar et al. 2016, Mohamed and
Abdelmoty 2016, Saleem 2018).
   There are a few significant contributions explicitly working on applying spatial-social
networks to indoor environments. ‘Check-inside’ improves indoor fingerprinting accuracy
by aggregating user smartphone sensors information and LBSN public information
together to create a multi-modal sensor-based semantic fingerprint of different locations
inside a mall (Elhamshary et al. 2017). ‘Find & Connect’ framework manages office shared
resources like meeting rooms and desks, based on the information gathered in an LBSN
among office employees (Wang et al. 2010, Zhu et al. 2010, Chin et al. 2011, 2012).
A conference management system has also been developed to associate researchers
based on their indoor location proximity (Chang et al. 2011, Xu et al. 2011, 2011). While
Check-inside is oriented to localization issues of indoor LBSNs, Find & Connect instead
studied the interactions between places and social interactions between friends to trigger
some recommendations. However, neither considers the underlying interconnected
structure of indoor environments and the underlying relations among indoor places
and users. They also do not explicitly model implicit traces of relations between users
and places. Furthermore, neither of them uses native graph-based databases in their
practical implementations.

3. Representation building blocks
Roughly speaking, there are two dominant graph data models: property graphs and
hypergraphs. While property graphs are made up of a set of nodes connected by
edges, where the edges have a direction associated with them, hypergraphs support
hyperedges that connect any number of given nodes for modeling n-ary relations.
A hypergraph can be represented with an isomorphically equivalent property graph,
but not vice versa (Akrawi 2010, Xiao 2014, Tarakci 2017). A simplicial complex is
a specific kind of hypergraph (Lin and Chiang 2005) that provides a robust set of analytical
tools that move beyond their ability to generalize traditional network and hypergraph
models, adding new ways to examine social processes (Greening et al. 2015).
    Simplices, as the simplest and most abstract form of geometric objects in algebraic
topology, are building blocks of simplicial complexes (Frank 2005, Fasy et al. 2014). An n-
dimensional simplex σno ¼ hv0 ; v1 ; . . . ; vn i which is called ann-simplex is determined by
n þ 1 nodes, representing n þ 1 reference objects, related to the main object o. A q-

is also a vertex of σno . So, the σqo vertex set is any non-empty subset of the σno vertex set.
A set of relevant simplices properly joined with all their faces is called a simplicial complex

    The intersection of two simplices is called their shared face. Two simplices are q-near if

4.1. The spatial subgraph data model
Among various symbolic, geometric, and semantic models for representing quantitative,
qualitative, and semantics aspects of an indoor environment’s spatial entities (Nagel 2014,
Yang 2015, Afyouni et al. 2016), a symbolic location-exit model, based on Li and Lee
(2008)’s work, was chosen as the spatial subgraph of indoor environments. This choice’s
motivation was that spatial-social network’s services fit relatively well with symbolic
models where the focus is on discrete places and associated semantic relationships rather
than precise geometric coordinates. The location-exit model facilitates the modeling of
topological connectivity between indoor places. This facilitation assists the model to
incorporate a network metric, developed by a simplex-based approach introduced in
section 4.3.1, representing the ease of access between pairs of indoor places.

   Defining indoor places is the starting issue and one of the challenging ones
when modeling an indoor environment for a spatial-social network. An indoor
place can be defined as a coherent indoor subspace based on structural, func­
tional, and organizational criteria (Zlatanova et al. 2014, Sithole and Zlatanova
2016, Diakité et al. 2017, Diakité and Zlatanova 2018, Pang et al. 2018, Yan et al.
2019). At the implementation level, representing an indoor place for a spatial-
social network depends on the location, number, and granularity of the position­
ing sensors available in the environment, which is not an issue considered by this
research. We assumed an indoor positioning system in the environment with the
functionality of mapping signals into our location-exit model’s symbolic reference
system (e.g. RFID, Bluetooth, QR codes). We considered the most homogeneous

4.2. The spatially augmented social subgraph data model
The social subgraph’s User nodes represent the users of an indoor spatial-social network. If
two users know each other, there is a KNOWS edge connecting them. This relatively simple
social subgraph is spatially augmented by check-ins information of the spatial-social
network. When a user acts in a place, a time-stamped Check-in node is added to the socio-
spatial graph. This new Check-in node is connected to the corresponding User and Place
nodes through CHECK-IN edges. Figure 4 shows a part of the spatially augmented social
subgraph implemented through testbed experimentation. User 926, illustrated by a blue
circle, made check-ins to BUREAU 216 and SALLE TP 335. Check-in nodes and edges,
illustrated by small green circles and corresponding lines connecting check-in nodes to
the user and the places, represent these check-ins. User 926 knows seven other users,
illustrated with other blue circles related through KNOWS edges to User 926.


4.3. Simplex-based representation of user and place profiles
As instantiations of the user model, user profiles are the building blocks of an online social
network capturing direct user interactions. Spatial-social networks enriched these profiles
with a comprehensive view of users’ association with places to denote users’ spatial and
semantic preferences (Lee and Ye 2014, Mohamed and Abdelmoty 2017, Tarakci 2017).
Correspondingly, spatial-social networks facilitate the representation of place profiles that
reflect users acting in them. We used simplices to represent user and place profiles, and
Q-analysis of simplicial complexes to model nested sequences of higher-order relations
among users and places in an indoor spatial-social network. There are four types of
simplices in our modeling approach, which are as follows:

4.3.1. Simplex-based enrichment of spatial subgraph
Representing indoor places and their connecting elements with access-oriented place
simplices enriches the spatial subgraph with a topological measure for ease of access
between indoor places. A physically distant place to a reference place can be topologically
near to the reference place if it is well connected to in-between places (Shaw and Sui
2020). The topological nearness of two indoor places is cognitively more valuable than the
physical position-based distance in many cases (Sun et al. 2014, Werner and Feld 27–30
Oct. 2014 Busan, Korea, 230–238, Si and Arikawa 2015, Sithole and Zlatanova 2016).
Intuitively, if an indoor place has many exits, it is more difficult for a person to choose
the right door leading to his destination. Conversely, the existence of more paths between
two indoor places makes it easier for a person to go from one place to another (Li and Lee
2008). Q-analysis of access-oriented indoor places simplicial complex can reveal the
connective strength between two indoor places. The access-oriented connective strength
between two given places denotes the degree of easiness for a person to reach one place
from the other. Based on the Sorensen-Dice measure (Dice 1945, Sørensen 1948), Bray–
Curtis dissimilarity measure (Bray and Curtis 1957), and the works of Li (Li 2007, Li and Lee
2008, Li and Kwong 2010), we adapted and retained a mobility measure representing the
ease of access between two access-oriented place simplices σia and σjb in an indoor
environment as follows:

4.3.2. Simplex-based enrichment of similarity and relevance measures
The study of pairwise similarities amongst user or place profiles is crucial for spatial-social
network services for recommendations and community detection. This study is based on
the extended First Law of Geography: ‘everything/everyone is related to everything/
everyone else, but near things/ones are more related than distant things/ones;
Nearness and relationship can be considered a matter of geographical and social network
distance, relationship, and interaction’ (Luo and MacEachren 2014). People who visited
similar places are more likely to share similar interests and preferences (Zheng 2011).
Similarly, when similar people act in some places, those places are more likely to be similar
in terms of functions, affordances, and organization (Zheng and Xie 2011, Bao et al. 2015).
Intuitively, the spatial-social similarity between two users indicates whether their activity
is performed in similar places. When two users are acting in similar places, this increases
the possible similarity between them. Similarly, the spatial-social similarity between two
places indicates to which degree some people are acting in the same in those places
(Kanza et al. 2017).
    Higher-order co-occurrence information provides a much richer data representation
than binary ones, showing patterns across places and people that can improve spatial-
social information retrieval (Li 2007, Ganiz et al. 2009, Li and Kwong 2010). While applying
first-order co-occurrences has been the most common way for spatial-social query expan­
sion and similarity analysis (Wu et al. 2018), Q-analysis of simplicial complexes can
investigate traces of higher-order co-occurrences of users and places using its multilevel
structure. When main objects and reference objects of a simplicial complex are in
a predictive relation, q-connectivity is a similarity measure between objects based on
their shared reference objects (Legrand 2002, Iravani 2006). To counter the deficiency of
Sorensen-Dice measure (Dice 1945, Sørensen 1948) and Bray–Curtis dissimilarity measure
(Bray and Curtis 1957) in modeling higher-order co-occurrence, and based on the works of
Li (Li 2007, Li and Lee 2008, Li and Kwong 2010) we adapted and retained a simplex-based
similarity measure (SSM) between two simplices σia and σjb as follows:

                     SSM σia ; σjb ¼                                                        (3)

SSM evaluates the spatial-social similarities between pairs of user-oriented place simplicial
complex. It denotes how many similar users made check-ins to two given places and how
many similar places two given users made check-ins. SSM can also show the spatial-social
relevance between a user-oriented user simplex and a place-oriented user simplex. This
feature indicates how relevant a place is to a user, based on their related users’ check-ins.
SSM is entirely based on the simplicial analysis of the relationships between users and
indoor places, so it does not need any tags given by users, as in geo-folksonomy models
(Mohamed and Abdelmoty 2017), or any additional attributes to derive similarity measures.
   The mentioned approach of simplex-based enrichment updates some of the socio-
spatial graph’s edges. UPU2 edges are updated with SSM property showing the spatial-
social similarity between corresponding pairs of users. Similarly, PUP3 edges are updated
with SSM property revealing the spatial-social similarity between corresponding pairs of
places. Finally, UUP4 edges are updated with SSM property revealing the spatial-social
relevance between a place and a user.

5. Experiments and evaluation
The proposed model was experimentally implemented by simulating two sample spatial-
social networks to show the model’s capabilities and efficiency. The first experiment was
based on a fictive spatial-social network composed of 29 users and their relations and 69
places on the second floor of our testbed building, the ISEN building at Brest, France.
The second one was based on another fictive spatial-social network consisting of 1108
users extracted from the work of Yin et al. (2017) and 268 places of our testbed building.
Two hundred fifty-six check-in records during two working days and 5200 check-in
records during five working days were simulated for the first and second experiments,
respectively.
    Neo4j (Neo4j Inc 2018) was chosen as the primary platform for our model’s implementa­
tion. Neo4j is an open-source native graph database written in Java and Scala. It supports
easy and fast traversing and navigating connected nodes through relationships between
them by providing an easy-to-understand but powerful query language, known as Cypher
(Francis et al. 2018), that facilitates creating and retrieving data based on graph pattern-
matching. The choice of Neo4j, amongst other graph databases like AllegroGraph, ArangoDB,
InfiniteGraph, and OrientDB, was based on its advantages in simplicity, powerful query
language, multiple architecture options, flexible schema, and scalability (Das et al. 2020,
Fernandes and Bernardino 2018). The construction of simplices and Q-analysis of simplicial
complexes was done using several Python scripts with the help of Py2neo (Nigel 2018).


5.1. Qualitative evaluation of simplex-based enrichment
The use of SSM as similarity and relevance measures is an emergent property of using
simplicial structures in the proposed modeling approach. Q-analysis of simplicial com­
plexes enriches the socio-spatial graph by capturing traces of higher-order users’ and
places’ co-occurrences. This consideration reveals latent relations that are not evident by
considering binary relations (Li and Kwong 2010). The capability of revealing latent
relations provides a more qualitatively reasonable answer to some spatial-social network
services like place recommendation and friendship predictions.
   A new place is recommended to a user under a specific context based on their
historical mobility traces and their connections to other users through the spatial-social
network’s location recommendation service. Assessing similarity and relevance between
users and places is one of the requirements of location recommendation approaches in
spatial-social network analysis (Yang et al. 2019). Simplex-based enrichment facilitates
location recommendation through two approaches. Each place is connected to other
places through PUP edges, representing the similarity between each pair of places based
on similar people acting in them. So, a user can find out how similar every place is to his
requested place. On the other hand, each user is connected to each place through a UUP
edge. This edge represents how relevant a place is to a user. The relevance is calculated
based on mobility traces of the given user’s friends.
   For instance, and during the process of place recommendation, if user 66 intends to
find how similar BUREAU 015 is to BUREAU 311 Ter, there is no same user who made check-
ins to both places in our testbed implementation. So, these two places do not seem to be
similar. On the contrary, the relatively significant value of the PUP edge’s similarity

CHECK-IN relations. Figure 7 shows traces of CHECK-IN relations between BUREAU 015 and

5.2. Quantitative evaluation of the graph-based implementation
Two implemented networks have been tested against a selective combination of com­
mon spatial-social queries that previously discussed and studied as benchmark queries
(Armenatzoglou et al. 2013, 2015, Brown et al. 2014, Škrášek 2015, Saleem et al. 2016,
Zhang et al. 2016, Zhang and Lu 2017, Sun and Sarwat 2018, Shim et al. 2018). In these
queries, SCSD represents the social separation degree between two users. It means the
number of hops needed to reach from a user to another in the social subgraph. If two
users know each other, they are considered friends. FOAF stands for being at least the
given user’s friend of a friend with a given SCSD. The benchmark queries are as follows:

  ● Q1: Get top-k places having most access to other places limited by distance
  ● Q2: Get top-k nearest friends or FOAFs with the given SCSD over a period based on
     their check-ins
  ● Q3: Get the top-k most similar strangers based on their spatial-social similarity
  ● Q4: Get top-k nearest most similar places to the given place based on spatial-social
     similarity
  ● Q5: Get top-k nearest most relevant places to the given user based on spatial-social
     relevance
  ● Q6: Top-k similar friends or FOAFs with the given SCSD of the given user based on
     their spatial-social similarity

These benchmark queries were processed using simplex-based similarity, relevance, and
mobility distance measures, introduced in section 4.3 and were qualitatively proven to be
efficient in section 5.1. Q4 and Q5 were processed using the nested loops algorithm for
processing skyline queries (Sohail et al. 2018, Amr and El-Tazi 2018). Other top-k queries
were processed using sorting based on the query’s similarity/relevance criterion and
graph traversal algorithms supporting SCSDs, FOAFs, and distances.

    Roughly speaking, and by splitting combined queries into their most basic compart­
ments, the benefits of having index-free adjacency can also be seen in the time complex­
ity. The Neo4j queries’ time complexity is OðkÞ, where k is the number of nodes in the

number of result nodes, and n is the number of graph nodes. Index-free adjacency makes
Neo4j look at the nodes directly connected to the start node with the cost of Oð1Þ. It
makes the lookup instant, and the only nodes looked at are the nodes in the final result.
On the other hand, PostgreSQL should perform a table lookup for each end of the start
node, being Oð2 log nÞ: This should be performed for all of the k nodes in the result.
    In conclusion, one can say that when dealing with graph-related operations that
should take advantage of both the underlying placed-based and social-based properties
of an indoor environment, a graph database offers a series of valuable manipulation
capabilities. A graph database reduces implementation complexity and impedance mis­
match because most concepts in the domain of spatial-social networks can be concep­
tually represented as an embedded graph. Moreover, as a spatial-social graph is likely to
change dynamically, a graph database has higher flexibility than a relational database
when storing social graph data. The results also show that queries written in Cypher are
also much shorter, easier to understand, and more intuitive to code than SQL.


6. Conclusion
This paper provides a new modeling approach to represent spatial-social networks in indoor
environments through an algebraic topological graph-based data model. The model inherits
simplex-based modeling’s theoretical advantages and graph-based representation’s practical
benefits. Simplex-based enrichment of the socio-spatial graph enables the model to derive
latent similarities and relevancies between the spatial-social network’s users and places by
considering traces of higher-order relations between indoor places and the network’s users.
Simplex-based enrichment of spatial subgraph also facilitates the definition of an indoor-
specific spatial measure representing the ease of access between indoor places that can be
applied in spatial-social queries’ spatial components. On the other hand, the interconnected­
ness of the socio-spatial graph provides a logical sense for using graph databases to fill the
gap between the conceptual and physical representations and facilitate traverse-based
pattern-matching queries. Testbed experimentations illustrate the potential of the whole
approach for data manipulation, performing social, spatial, and spatial-social queries.
    However, our modeling approach must consider additional factors such as user roles,
places affordances, temporal patterns, and indoor activities. Another future research
should consider the spatiotemporal dimension to make spatial-social network services
more appropriate. To more deeply consider place affordances (Yang 2015) and activities
(Fan 2017), OWL ontologies and additional reasoning capabilities should be better
integrated with our graph databases’ underlying principles. As well, the spatial subgraph
still does not consider hierarchical representations of an indoor space that can reflect
additional functional and structural properties of the spatial social network’s representa­
tion. Finally, we also plan to investigate spatial-social relationships’ effect on an indoor
environment’s spatial hierarchy structuration.
    Besides the mentioned conceptual modeling issues, indoor spaces encompass sym­
bolic representations that require novel index schemas from a database point of view.
2534       M. RAHIMI ET AL.


With an eye on previous works using R-tree indexes for spatio-temporal range queries
(Jensen et al. 2009) or a semantic hierarchical tree structure for manipulating semantic
trajectories at the query level (Lin et al. 2016), an indoor specific indexing scheme should
be implemented in our future work.

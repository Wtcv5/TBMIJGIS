1. Introduction
The principal objective of urban physical examination, alternatively termed urban health
assessments, is to quantify and analyze the city’s spatial configuration, demographic dis­
tribution, resource allocation, and environmental quality. These factors are collectively
referred to as ‘vital signs’ and are used to ascertain the health status and developmental
trajectories of urban systems (Barton and Grant 2013; Friel et al. 2011; Galea and Vlahov
2005; Harpham 2009; Vardoulakis, Dear, and Wilkinson 2016). This process is data-
driven, relying on a spectrum of quantified indicators that reflect these urban vital
signs for the monitoring, diagnosis, early warning, and maintenance of urban develop­
ment (Allen et al. 2017; Andries et al. 2019; Pineo et al. 2018).
    Internationally, urban physical examination predominantly entails the establishment
of a comprehensive evaluation indicator system of urban physical signs (Corburn 2004;
Sharifi and Murayama 2014). Specifically, by collecting data on urban infrastructure,
public services, social economy, ecological environment, cultural features and other
aspects, combined with empirical materials such as questionnaires and social feedback,
and applying methods like structural decomposition, inventory statistics, model calcu­
lation, standard setting, health status classification, and comprehensive measurement,
a multi-dimensional comparative analysis and evaluation are conducted on all aspects
of the urban ‘living organism’ (Biswas et al. 2021; Dempsey et al. 2011; He et al. 2018).
Since the 1990s, the international scientific community has consistently placed significant
emphasis on pre-implementation appraisal, process monitoring, and post-implemen­
tation evaluation within the scene of urban development planning (Naess 2001; Yigitcan­
lar and Teriman 2015). It not only gauges the degree of congruence between the
implementation outcomes and the planning objectives but also accords greater attention
to sundry urban health issues that impinge upon the implementation process. The
United Kingdom, Japan and the United States have all formulated strategic planning
objectives, constructed quantifiable indicators and thus formed the core content of moni­
toring and evaluation (Sharifi and Murayama 2014). Then, centering around these indi­
cators, they have aggregated multi-source spatio-temporal data that are dynamic and
timely to build a monitoring and evaluation database. Eventually, by combining quanti­
tative and qualitative indicator evaluation methods, they have provided strong support
for the implementation of planning and the action plans of local governments (Geneletti
et al. 2017). Nevertheless, this process also faces the challenges of width of data sources,
the depth of indicator diagnosis, and the efficacy of comprehensive data application
(Childers et al. 2014). In February 2017, the Chinese government proposed the establish­
ment of an assessment mechanism for urban physical examination, and since then many
cities in China have carried out related work (Zhang, He, and Chen 2021). Among them,
as one of the assessment items of urban physical examination, urban greenways can
promote green travel and reduce carbon emissions, so their construction and develop­
ment have received much attention (Semeraro et al. 2021; Van Oijstaeijen, Van Passel,
and Cools 2020). In general, urban physical examination rely on strategic planning orien­
tation, evaluation index, and spatiotemporal data collection (Cui et al. 2024; Ren et al.
2024). However, the strategic planning objectives of urban physical examination are
different, which makes it difficult to make horizontal comparisons of assessment indi­
cators and carry out multi-level vertical analyses between ‘objectives-indicators-data’
                                                 INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF DIGITAL EARTH      3


(He et al. 2023). Furthermore, in practice, too much attention is paid to the urban phys­
ical examination indicators themselves, ignoring the semantic association between the
indicators and data, which in turn makes it difficult to explore the intrinsic connection
and potential value of urban physical signs (Chen et al. 2022). In addition, the current
urban physical examination lacks a spatiotemporal data model based on application
scenarios, which makes it difficult to reflect its spatiotemporal characteristics and
changes (Cook and Karvonen 2024). Therefore, high-quality urban physical examination
not only needs to consider the complex interactions between planning objectives and
assessment indicators, but also needs to address the differences in dimensionality and
granularity of different spatiotemporal data.
   In recent years, there has been a gradual shift in the urban physical examination meth­
odology from the utilization of socio-economic statistical data to the incorporation of
multi-source spatial and temporal data, such as remotely sensed imagery, points of inter­
est, human tracks, and street view images (Giles-Corti, Lowe, and Arundel 2020; Masó
et al. 2019; Stokes and Seto 2019). It is not sufficient to store these voluminous multidi­
mensional data sets, but it is also imperative to guarantee the correlation and semantic
consistency between the data, to facilitate the intricate analysis and decision-making pro­
cesses. However, current research mostly relies on traditional relational or non-relational
databases, such as files, key-value pairs, attribute tables, etc., which are only suitable for
dealing with structured data and simple relational queries, and lack the organization and
management of spatiotemporal data (Codd 1970). And the current spatiotemporal data
model of urban physical examination has not yet been developed, which makes it difficult
to represent highly interconnected data and complex semantic relationship networks.
This restricts the deep integration and analysis capabilities at the level of urban
medical examination spatiotemporal data, and it is difficult to meet the data organization
and management needs. It is mainly reflected in the following three aspects: (1) Variable
Application Scenarios: The urban physical examination encompasses multiple stake­
holders and variables, presenting challenges in designing and executing effective proto­
cols (Wirth et al. 2014); (2) Insufficient Knowledge Articulation: The assessments involve
knowledge covering numerous themes, connotations, knowledge points, and indicators.
This renders the expression, understanding, and visualization of such knowledge a
complex task (Sajjad, Chan, and Chopra 2021); (3) Intricate Data Associations: The
data dealt with in assessments have spatiotemporal and semantic linkages and are charac­
terized by high-dimensional dynamics. This complicates tasks like data analysis, mining,
reasoning, updating, and traceability (Hamdi et al. 2022). Consequently, the effective
integration of the semantic and logical representation of urban vital signs, the manage­
ment of the entire lifecycle of spatiotemporal data, and the provision of scenario-specific
presentations to amplify the application efficacy of data and knowledge have emerged as
pivotal issues in contemporary research.
   The Knowledge Graph (KG), an advanced information organization technology, uti­
lizes graph databases to interconnect multi-source data via entities (nodes) and relation­
ships (edges), thereby constructing a unified data view conducive to complex data
analysis and knowledge discovery (Abu-Salih 2021; Ji et al. 2021). It links dispersed
data points into a network, which not only facilitates comprehensive assessment of
urban vital signs but also uncovers implicit knowledge from massive data through
reasoning and analysis, providing deeper insights and decision-making support
4      S. WANG ET AL.


(Chadzynski et al. 2021; Janowicz et al. 2022). Preliminary progress has been achieved in
the application of KG for to urban spatiotemporal data. For example, WorldKG has
linked OpenStreetMap’s geospatial data with knowledge from various fields by aligning
with other KGs like Wikidata and DBpedia, enhancing data interoperability (Dsouza
et al. 2021). The UK’s National Digital Twin (NDT) unifies disparate data sources
through a semantic model, integrating real-time data streams and computational
agents to form a knowledge network for supporting sophisticated analyses (Akroyd
et al. 2021). Moreover, the CityGML-KG framework transforms 3D urban model data
into a KG, integrating with sources such as OpenStreetMap and enhancing data semantic
expression (Ding et al. 2024). Domestically, researchers are actively applying KG in urban
planning and management. For instance, the UrbanKG system combines street view
imagery, satellite imagery, and user behavior data to construct a knowledge graph that
facilitates the integration of multimodal datasets, encompassing text, images, and
sequences (Liu et al. 2023). UrbanGPT integrates large language models (LLMs) into
spatiotemporal data analysis, enabling the model to handle not only natural language
but also understand and predict complex spatiotemporal dynamics (Li et al. 2024). Fur­
thermore, some scholars have introduced KG technology into urban physical examin­
ation and developed KG-driven collaborative methods for multi-inspection tasks,
realizing the synergy of ‘data-indicator-calculation’ among multiple types of urban phys­
ical examinations (Wang et al. 2022). This has brought new tools and demonstrated great
application potential. For example, the utilization of RDF triple stores and native graph
database storage, such as Jena, RDF4J, Neo4j, etc., has enhanced the integration and
analysis capabilities at the data level (Fernandes and Bernardino 2018; Wang et al.
2019). It should be noted that although current research can support RDF graphs and
attribute maps to achieve spatiotemporal data management based on knowledge
graphs, however, these studies are still insufficient to support the demand for in-depth
integration and analysis applications of urban physical signs (Chen, Liu, and Tian
2022; Krishnan, Dziedzic, and Elmore 2018; Liu et al. 2021a; 2021b). Specifically, when
dealing with spatiotemporal data, the integration of multi-source data is often incom­
plete, resulting in data fragmentation and making it difficult to comprehensively and
accurately reflect the complex situation of urban physical examination. In terms of
knowledge mining, most studies fail to construct an effective conceptual model to
deeply explore the potential relationships among data, leading to a lack of in-depth scien­
tific basis for planning decisions (Akroyd et al. 2021) Additionally, with respect to result
presentation and visualization, current studies have difficulty in intuitively and effectively
conveying key information to urban planners and decision-makers, which is not condu­
cive to multi-departmental collaboration and public participation (Chen et al. 2023).
    In view of the above, this paper proposes a knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal
data model for the urban physical examination. Initiating from application scenarios,
it aims to integrate multi-source heterogeneous spatiotemporal data more effectively
through knowledge graph and explore the complex relationships among spatiotemporal
data, to optimize the organization and management of spatiotemporal data for urban
physical examination knowledge services. The model adopts a three-layer architecture
of ‘semantics-data-scenario’, which constructs the urban physical examination knowl­
edge system, manages the whole process spatiotemporal data of urban physical examin­
ation and provides scenario-based spatiotemporal expression. This paper presents the
                                              INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF DIGITAL EARTH    5


conceptual and logical models of the proposed model, and provides relevant example and
explanations by taking the urban greenway construction as an instance.

(1) Currently, most urban physical examination spatiotemporal data models concen­
    trate on the design of new indicators and conceptual discussions. Although progress
    has been made in the organization and association of data and semantics in urban
    physical examination knowledge graphs, the requirements of urban physical exam­
    ination application scenarios have not been fully considered. This paper proposes a
    knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal data model for urban physical examination.
    The model integrates the knowledge in the semantic layer and the data in the data
    layer according to the requirements of application scenarios. Then, it presents
    them in an intuitive and highly interactive visual manner through the scenario
    layer to obtain customized knowledge services.
(2) Design a logical model integrating UML diagrams and hypergraph ontology. This
    logical model represents high-order relations as hyperedges, simplifies the structure
    of UML models, and enhances the accuracy of expressing multi-dimensional
    relations.
(3) Conduct model validation with the example of Guangzhou greenway construc­
    tion. Based on the model, the example identifies and diagnoses the problems
    in the construction of Guangzhou greenways, puts forward targeted strategies,
    and improves decision-making efficiency by means of visualization, thus provid­
    ing a visual knowledge service example for the management of urban greenway
    construction.


2. General framework
Within the urban physical examination spatiotemporal data management, conventional
Entity-Relationship (ER) modeling approaches encounter limitations in scalability. They
struggle to capture the interconnections between urban physical examination spatiotem­
poral data and associated knowledge domains or application scenarios (Malinowski and
Zimányi 2008; Sharifi 2021; Teorey, Yang, and Fry 1986; Zhou et al. 2021). Thus, to
enhance the efficient organization and management of urban physical examination
spatiotemporal data, this study introduces a knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal
data model for urban physical examination, as shown in Figure 1. Starting from appli­
cation scenarios, this model builds a comprehensive urban physical examination knowl­
edge framework, manages the entire spatiotemporal data lifecycle, and provides scenario-
specific spatiotemporal expressions. It integrates various spatiotemporal data types and
relational structures into the unified framework.
   Firstly, this paper proposes a three-layer framework conceptual model of ‘semantics-
data-scenario’ around the application scenario requirements. Within this framework, the
semantics layer abstracts and defines the spatiotemporal semantics of urban vital signs.
The data layer is responsible for integrating and managing the spatiotemporal data
across the whole process. The scenario layer enables the conversion of abstract data
and knowledge into perceptible and visual representations of urban development narra­
tives via scenario-specific spatiotemporal presentations.

   Secondly, this paper centers around application scenarios and constructs a logical
model encompassing scenario identifiers, knowledge systems, and evidence data, and
integrating logical relationships such as inclusion, inheritance, and extension. The
logical model utilizes Unified Modeling Language (UML) diagrams to depict the
logical structures of data, knowledge, and scenarios along with their spatiotemporal attri­
butes. Concurrently, to enhance the expression of complex associations among spatio­
temporal data, an ontology model based on a hypergraph structure is introduced. It
represents higher-order relationships through hypernodes and hyperedges to optimize
the expression of complex associations between spatiotemporal data.
   Finally, this paper employs urban greenway construction as a case study for knowl­
edge visualization and expression to demonstrate the application efficacy of the model.
The case offers descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, and programmatic greenway knowl­
edge services by gathering, integrating, and analyzing relevant data to comprehensively
assess the state of urban greenway construction.


3. Conceptual modeling
Urban physical examination spatiotemporal data is multi-faceted, covering multiple
types, time periods, and media. It encompasses natural background, population
dynamics, environmental quality, etc. The forms include image, text, table, and video
data, laying a solid foundation for in-depth urban physical examination analyses.
                                                   INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF DIGITAL EARTH       7


   This paper differentiates between data and knowledge. Urban physical examination
data refers to the spatiotemporal data collected from all aspects and stages of a city,
such as basic attributes, functional structure, development status, and potential risks.
It involves the city’s monitoring, diagnosis, early warning, and maintenance stages
(Wu et al. 2024). Urban physical examination knowledge is the result of processing
and analyzing such data, including descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, and scenario-
based knowledge about the city, as well as knowledge about its strengths, weaknesses,
problems, suggestions, and countermeasures (Chen et al. 2020; 2021; Chen, Liu, and
Tian 2022). The data and knowledge of urban physical examination are closely inter­
twined and mutually interactive. Data serves as the basis for knowledge, provides data
support for scenario-based applications, while knowledge represents the distillation
and application of data and guides scenario-based applications.
   Firstly, this paper presents a triple conceptual model consisting of semantic, data, and
scenario aspects to guide the development of a knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal
data model. As shown in Figure 2, these layers correspond respectively to the knowledge,
data, and application aspects of urban physical examination.
   In detail, the semantic layer proposes a hierarchical structure for urban physical exam­
ination, including themes, connotations, knowledge points, and indicators. It constructs
a comprehensive four-tier urban physical examination knowledge system, integrating
both semantic and data entities into a unified framework. This aims to enhance the
knowledge density and logical coherence of the data, strengthens the expression of
data significance and interconnections, and provides a solid knowledge foundation for
the scenario layer. The data layer organizes and refines relevant data knowledge accord­
ing to the four key stages of urban physical examination: monitoring, diagnosis, warning,
and maintenance. It aims to support the calculation of evaluation metrics, comprehen­
sive diagnosis, causal analysis, and the improvement of irregularities in urban physical
examination, thereby providing substantial data support for the scenario layer. The scen­
ario layer instantiates the knowledge and data from the semantic and data layers, and rea­
lizes the visual representation of knowledge services in specific scenarios, facilitating the
provision of urban physical examination knowledge services.

3.1. Semantic layer
This paper formulates a hierarchical urban physical examination knowledge system that
consists of four levels: ‘theme-connotation-knowledge point-indicator’, as shown in
Figure 3. The urban semantic and data entities are abstracted into a structured model,
which facilitates the semantic modeling of urban physical examination concepts, their
interrelations, and their spatiotemporal characteristics. This methodological improve­
ment enhances data interpretability and relationship-expression capacity, ensuring align­
ment with the diverse knowledge demands of different urban physical examination
scenarios. The formal representation of the semantic layer is described as follows.


                                      S = (ST , SC , SK , SI )                                 (1)


In the equation, the term S with subscripted quotations (T, C, K, I) refers to a semantic
set. ST indicates the theme node, SC represents the connotation node, SK , stands for the
knowledge node, and SI denotes the indicator node. The specific details of these four
levels of nodes are presented below.

3.1.1. Theme
The theme layer is the highest level of the knowledge system of urban physical examin­
ation, which provides a macro perspective and framework for urban physical examin­
ation. This paper takes Guangzhou city as an example, and selects the themes of
Ecological Livability, innovation dynamism and health and comfort, etc., as delineated
in Table 1. Based on the analysis of the relevant policies, plans and indicator index of
urban physical examination, this paper explores the city’s development goals and value
orientation, and then adopts the form of ontology to define the classification, hierarchical
structure, attributes and relationships of the themes of urban physical examination. The
formal representation of the theme is presented as follows:

                                               T = (TH , TA , TR )                                                        (2)

Where, T represents the set of themes. TH refers to the theme’s structure, TA indicates the
theme’s attributes, and TR signifies the theme’s relationships. Specifically, TH is defined as
a tree data structure to represent the hierarchical relationships among themes. Each node
within this structure represents a theme, and the edges between nodes indicate the
parent-child relationships between themes. For example, the theme of Ecological Livabil­
ity could contain sub-themes such as Ecological Environmental Protection and living
Space. These sub-themes are manifested as nodes within TH and are linked to the Eco­
logical Livability theme node by edges. TA is dictionary data in the form of key-value
pairs, where the key is the name of the attribute related to the topic and the value is
the descriptive information or quantitative value of the corresponding attribute, e.g.
{’Eco-livability’: {’Topic definition’: … , ‘Resident satisfaction’: 0.8}}. TR represents the
types of relationships or the intensity of associations among themes. For example, if
there is a positive correlation between ‘Environmental Livability’ and ‘Convenient Trans­
port’, the value of the corresponding element in TR would be 0.7, indicating the degree of
relationship between the two.

3.1.2. Connotation
Connotation is the second level, which provides in-depth understanding and analysis for
the physical examination of the city, reflecting the connotation and relationship of the
city’s development. This paper analyses the points of concern according to the theme
and indicators, and combines relevant literature and cases to condense the basic conno­
tations of urban physical examination, as shown in Table 2. The connotations are related
to each other. For example, the residential space connotation under the Ecological Liva­
bility theme is closely linked to the community service connotation under the Health and
Comfort theme. The connotation is formally expressed as:
                                    CT = (T, Cj ), j = 1, 2, 3 . . . , n                               (3)
Where, CT denotes the connotations related to theme T, while Cj indicates the different
connotative features subsumed under a particular theme, covering three dimensions:
environment, society, and economy. Specifically, Cj assumes the form of dictionary
data in key-value pairs. The key corresponds to the name of the connotation character­
istic dimension, and the value embodies the dimensional information of the correspond­
ing attribute, covering three dimensions of the urban ecological environment (such as
green and low-carbon development paradigm, ecological environmental conservation),
social livelihood (such as living space, community service guarantee), and economic
development (such as open development, innovative development).

edge system, providing specific contents and dimensions for urban physical examin­
ations, reflecting the current state of development and existing issues in cities. Based

examination, the classification, hierarchical structure, concepts, attributes, and relation­
ships of knowledge points are defined in the ontological form, thereby delineating
specific knowledge contents and dimensions. A part of urban physical examination
knowledge points is presented in Table 3.

edge points are further divided into three subcategories: Ecological and Environmental
Protection, Green and Low-Carbon Development, and Living Space. Each subcategory

3.2.2. Diagnosis phase
The diagnostic phase of the physical examination of the city uses spatiotemporal analysis
and mathematical and statistical methods to measure and evaluate the level of indicators
for various aspects of urban development. The data in this phase are based on the indi­
cator results of the data from the monitoring phase and involve three data types: numeri­
cal, labeled and graphical.
   Diagnostic numerical data are dynamically updated and reflect real-time changes in
urban development. The attributes include the diagnostic name, the target value, and
the relationships used for data updating, such as data sources, computational procedures,
and evaluation metrics. Diagnostic labels, on the other hand, reveal urban conditions and
regional characteristics, showing the horizontal and vertical hierarchical differentiation of
the data. The attributes of the labels detail the hierarchical structure and categorical
characteristics of the data, along with the data aggregation operations through relation­
ships such as summarization, averaging, and sorting. Then the diagnostic graphics
present the city’s strengths, problems, and potentials in the form of intuitive charts. Its
attributes describe the type and evaluation criteria of the graphs, as well as the complex
influencing, facilitating and constraining relationships between the data. Ultimately,
through diagnosis, it is possible to more accurately grasp the dynamic trend of urban devel­
opment and identify the level of development and characteristics of each region, thereby
providing scientific decision support for the warning and decision-making stages.

3.2.3. Early warning phase
The early warning stage is based on the resultant data of the diagnostic stage and uses
data mining and reasoning methods to further analyze the abnormal indicators
present in the city physical examination. The data in this stage are the early warning
results of the physical examination of the city and contain three data types: events,
causes and recommendations.
   Event data reveal key issues in urban development, such as diseases, pitfalls and
opportunities, through the analysis of anomalous indicators, with attributes that
include the risk and value of the event, as well as relationships between the data, includ­
ing early warning, intervention, and improvement measures.
   Cause data, on the other hand, identifies the root causes of anomalous events through
correlation and causal analyses, with attributes describing the characteristics and predic­
tions of the causes, as well as the relationships between the data, such as triggering,
propagation, and blocking.
18      S. WANG ET AL.


   Suggested data make appropriate suggestions for abnormal events and causes, with
hierarchical interaction and stage adjustment characteristics, while attributes relate to
the stages, assessment and optimization of suggestions, as well as feedback and adjust­
ment relationships between data, such as input, output and update. By further investi­
gating data mining and predictive reasoning, the warning phase could identify and
respond to potential risks in urban development in a timely manner, and provide tar­
geted recommendations to provide a scientific basis for strategies in the maintenance
phase.

3.2.4. Maintenance phase
The maintenance phase is based on the resultant data from the early warning phase to
optimize and manage the anomalous indicators and to form a comprehensive manage­
ment strategy and corresponding action plan to promote urban development. The types
of data in this phase include case studies, strategies and actions. The case studies include
both successful and unsuccessful comprehensive governance cases, which have been col­
lected and analyzed in the context of urban development. They serve to highlight typical
cases, lessons learned, and the significance of these cases for future reference. The strat­
egy represents the comprehensive urban governance strategies developed for abnormal
indicators through optimization models, decision support, and multi-objective planning,
such as urban improvement measures and optimization targets. The action measures
reflect the comprehensive governance action reports for urban development. These are
generated through the processes of execution supervision, effectiveness evaluation, and
feedback adjustment. They include details on the city’s implementation status, perform­
ance assessment, and feedback received.
   These maintenance outcome data not only reflect the city’s physiological condition in
aspects such as ecology, economy, and society but also demonstrate the level of develop­
ment in terms of efficiency, quality, and equity. They support and facilitate the city’s
achievement of sustainable and high-quality development goals. The attributes encom­
pass aspects such as maintenance data types, improvement measures, and potential
stimulation, as well as supervisory and evaluative relationships like execution, inspection,
feedback, improvement, and stimulation, providing a solid data foundation for urban
management and decision-making.

health scenarios, providing scenario-specific knowledge services aligned with diverse
application domains, including data services, analysis services, prediction services, evalu­
ation services, and optimization services for urban physical examination.
   In conclusion, the scenario knowledge is visually expressed in applications through
the utilization of appropriate visualization techniques, in accordance with the specific
forms of application, such as charts, maps, reports, and narratives derived from urban

This paper defines the various types of scenarios and their respective spatiotemporal

spectives. A scenario template is a customizable and easily expandable approach to
structuring scenarios. It encompasses semantic and data objects pertinent to the scenario,
such as domain, objectives, content, and form, as well as their interrelationships, includ­
ing causality, influence, and optimization.
    The term ‘semantic objects’ is used to describe the various semantic concepts that are
embedded within the urban physical examination knowledge graph. These include
themes, connotations, and knowledge points, which possess specific semantic meanings
and attribute characteristics. Such descriptions can be employed to delineate the multi­
farious aspects and levels of knowledge pertaining to urban physical examination. In con­
trast, data objects refer to the data elements that quantify and assess each stage and level
within the urban physical examination knowledge graph. These include indicators and
data, which are characterized by their numerical values and units of measurement. To
illustrate, scenario templates make direct use of themes, connotations, knowledge
points, indicators and data from the semantic layer for the purpose of pre-defining
knowledge content. They employ a variety of data forms, including textual, cartographic,
tabular, and pictorial, to express the logical and numerical connections between knowl­
edge and data. This allows for the presentation of the status and characteristics of a
specific aspect or level of urban physical examination.

4. Logical model and ontology
This paper focuses on application scenarios and employs Unified Modeling Language
(UML) diagrams to articulate the logical model of these scenarios, as shown in
Figure 4. It describes the logical structure and relationships of data, knowledge, and scen­
arios, as well as their spatiotemporal attributes. The urban physical examination knowl­
edge service encompasses multiple application sets, including monitoring, diagnostics,
warnings, maintenance, evaluation, and knowledge. Each application set has its own
expression of application scenarios, which is composed of attributes such as scenario
identifiers, knowledge systems, and evidence data, and possesses relationships of
inclusion, inheritance, and extension.
   To facilitate the expression and management of the spatiotemporal dimensions of
data, knowledge, and scenarios, this paper introduces three elements into each UML
class diagram: spatiotemporal tags, spatiotemporal indexes, and spatiotemporal versions.
Spatiotemporal tags are labels used to describe the spatiotemporal characteristics of data,
knowledge, and scenarios. They encompass features including time, location, scope, and

distribution. Each tag feature is characterized by attributes like type, value, precision, and
unit, as well as relationships such as sequence, position, distance, and direction.
   Spatiotemporal indexes are indexing structures used to retrieve spatiotemporal infor­
mation of data and knowledge applications. The above index types include spatiotem­
poral coding, spatiotemporal hashing, and spatiotemporal trees, each of which
possesses attributes such as type, value, length, and hierarchy. Moreover, these index
types exhibit relationships such as adjacency, containment, and intersection. Spatiotem­

spatiotemporal alterations pertaining to data, knowledge, and applications. Such

successor, branch, and merge.

offers a structured means of comprehending the intricacies of urban physical examin­

application scenarios. The incorporation of spatiotemporal elements augments the
model’s capability to cope with the dynamic nature of urban physical examination
data, thereby enabling more effective monitoring, analysis, and decision-making

5. Application case
To validate the effectiveness and practicality of the knowledge graph-based spatiotem­
poral data model for urban physical examination proposed in this paper, the construc­
tion of urban greenway in Guangzhou City was selected as an application case for in-
depth study. A prototype system was developed on the Python platform, using the
Django framework and the Neo4j graph database to implement the specific application
of the model. As an essential component of the urban ecological network, greenways not
only enhance the ecological quality of the city but also improve the living environment
for residents, making them an ideal subject for this experimental validation (Semeraro
et al. 2021; Van Oijstaeijen, Van Passel, and Cools 2020). Taking the construction scen­
ario of urban greenways in Guangzhou as an example, through the collection, inte­
gration, and analysis of relevant data related to greenway construction, the system
identifies the problems and risks in greenway construction and provides scientific
decision support.


5.1. Spatiotemporal data processing and storage
The processing and storage of spatiotemporal data for the urban greenway construction
in Guangzhou includes requirements analysis, data collection and collation, node con­
nection and index establishment, as well as hierarchical storage.
   Firstly, to effectively integrate and store spatiotemporal data related to urban greenway
construction, the system begins with a needs analysis to clarify the requirements for con­
solidating data and knowledge on urban greenways. Urban greenways, as a type of
nature-based urban green infrastructure, primarily rely on non-motorized transport
                                               INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF DIGITAL EARTH      25


and walking. They aim to meet residents’ needs for leisure, fitness, and tourism. The
spatiotemporal data involved cover multiple aspects, including ecology, landscape, trans­
portation, and society. These data should reflect the impact of greenways on residents’
lives, such as greenway density, greenway walking accessibility rate, and greenway
driving accessibility rate.
   Next, according to the data type requirements of each node in Formulas (1–7), spatio­
temporal data related to greenway construction is collected from different channels and
collated into data forms suitable for scene nodes, theme nodes, connotation nodes,
knowledge nodes, indicator nodes, etc. For example, spatial data of greenways is
extracted from the Geographic Information System, and after calculating indicator
data such as greenway density, it is stored in the indicator node and a correlation
relationship with the theme node Ecological Livability is established, indicating that
this indicator is an important basis for measuring the greenway construction situation
under the theme of Ecological Livability. Then, using the spatiotemporal data model
of urban physical examination based on knowledge graph, connections and indexes
between nodes are established to organize and express the relevant data and knowledge
of greenway construction. These scattered node data are integrated into a scene set
‘Scene’, which provides a basic data framework for the subsequent analysis and appli­
cation of the relevant data and knowledge of greenway construction, as shown in
Figure 6. Adopting a hierarchical storage architecture, the system stores the semantic
layer data of the knowledge graph, the raw data of the data layer, and the application
data of the scenario layer separately. A close association among these spatiotemporal
data is established through unique identifiers and index mechanisms.

5.1.1. Semantic layer
In the semantic layer, various semantic entities within the spatiotemporal data of urban
greenway construction are stored as nodes in the knowledge graph. Each node is assigned
a unique identifier within the neo4j graph database and stores the attribute information
related to the entity. For instance, an urban greenway density indicator node may possess
attributes such as the indicator name (urban greenway density), the calculation method
(e.g. a specific formula), and the unit (kilometers per square kilometer). Semantic
relationships between nodes are represented by edges. For example, an urban greenway
density indicator node and a diagnostic assessment knowledge point node are connected
by an edge labeled used for calculation, and the edge can also have attributes, such as the
weight of the relationship (indicating the strength of the relationship, e.g. the importance
weight of this indicator in certain calculations).

5.1.2. Data layer
The original data in the data layer is stored in appropriate file systems or key-value stores
based on its data type and source. Concurrently, data nodes are created in the knowledge
graph, and reference links pointing to the storage locations of the original data are estab­
lished in neo4j. This enables accurate retrieval and processing of the original data when
required. For example, for knowledge points, geographic information data, and online
video resources related to greenway construction nodes, a common indexing key
(such as the unique identifier of the greenway and the geographical area code) is estab­
lished in neo4j to quickly associate and retrieve the original data.

For the scenario layer data, in combination with application requirements, the configur­

the application scene are obtained through these nodes.

Neo4j stores a variety of relationships. These relationships encompass spatial relation­

etc.). The relationship attribute records information including the type of relationship,
the identifiers of the starting and ending nodes, and the weight of the relationship (indi­
cating the strength of the relationship).
   To enhance the efficiency of data querying and retrieval, multiple indexing mechan­

6. Discussion
It was found that the successful development for knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal
data model for urban physical examination is largely contingent upon numerous favor­
able conditions. These include the adoption of a ‘semantics-data-scenario’ three-layer
architecture for modeling, securing innovative interdisciplinary collaboration, and
achieving deep user engagement and feedback.


6.1. Applicability and localization of the model
In this study, a knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal data model for urban physical
examination with applicability and localization was successfully constructed through
structured semantic modeling, associative data integration, and scenario-oriented knowl­
edge representation. The model is not only capable of addressing the diversity and com­
plexity of urban physical examination data but also allows for customization according to
the unique needs of individual cities, accommodating various scales and localized urban
physical examination.
   Initially, to ensure that the knowledge graph comprehensively reflects all dimensions
of urban physical examination while avoiding excessive complexity, the model adopts a
four-tier structure: ‘theme-connotation-knowledge point-indicator’. This structure
divides the knowledge into logical layers, covering all necessary aspects of urban physical
examination and ensuring the model’s representativeness. Secondly, to maintain the
integrity of knowledge points while reducing redundant connections, the model uses a
hypergraph structure to simplify grouped or clustered knowledge. By representing
closely related knowledge as hypernodes and hyperedges, the model eliminates nodes
of redundant and weakly connected knowledge. This reduces model complexity and
improves computational efficiency. Thirdly, to provide customized knowledge based
on different application needs, the model converts abstract data and knowledge into
intuitive charts, maps, and reports through scenario-oriented knowledge representation.
This enhances the model’s interpretability.
   In the case of Guangzhou’s greenway construction, the model identified two key
issues: insufficient connectivity and uneven coverage of greenways. To address the con­
nectivity issue, it is recommended to prioritize greenway layout in new urban planning.
Natural geographical resources, such as water systems in Zengcheng District and moun­
tain ranges in Conghua District, should be fully utilized to plan new greenway routes.
When greenways are interrupted by rivers, highways, or other obstacles, bridge,
34      S. WANG ET AL.


tunnel, and pedestrian overpass projects should be actively implemented to ensure the
continuity of greenways. To address the issue of uneven coverage, community partici­
pation is recommended to gather residents’ needs and suggestions for greenway con­
struction. This will improve greenway usage and satisfaction. Additionally, a regular
evaluation mechanism for greenway maintenance should be established, and social
capital should be involved in maintenance efforts.
    In the above-mentioned case, the spatiotemporal data comes from multiple channels,
including remote sensing imagery, social media feedback, government statistics, and
online videos. While these data sources provided rich information, they also had limit­
ations and potential biases. First, remote sensing data had limited spatial resolution,
leading to inaccuracies in measuring greenways precisely. Government statistics,
though authoritative, were often not updated promptly, resulting in a lag between the
data and the actual state of greenway construction. Social media and online video data
could offer real-time feedback but lacked representativeness. For example, the voices
of the elderly or those from remote areas might be overlooked. Second, the absence of
detailed data and the presence of data biases made it difficult to conduct thorough quan­
titative analyses. Third, when assessing the model’s applicability across different cities
and regions, we found that urban physical examination goals and priorities are closely
tied to a city’s geographical location, economic structure, and socio-cultural background.
Therefore, the model’s customizable design allows urban planners and decision-makers
to select the most suitable evaluation indicators and knowledge nodes based on local con­
ditions, enabling localized application of the model.

6.3. Innovative interdisciplinary collaboration
The research and development for knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal data model
for urban physical examination is a complex and multifaceted process that inherently
involves a confluence of disciplines, including geographic information science, data
science, and urban planning. The successful execution of this study unequivocally
emphasizes the crucial and indispensable nature of close-knit collaboration and seamless
knowledge sharing within interdisciplinary teams (Frantzeskaki et al. 2020). In addition,
the establishment of effective mechanisms for interdisciplinary cooperation is essential to
taking research in this field to new heights.
   In the construction and practice application of the urban physical examination knowl­
edge graph, a diversified collaborative platform has been established, leveraging the
Guangzhou workstation of academicians as a catalyst. This platform is designed to
foster synergistic cooperation among the realms of urban planning, geographic infor­
mation science, data science, and artificial intelligence. By congregating experts and scho­
lars from diverse fields, it effectively dismantles traditional disciplinary boundaries and
barriers, thereby enabling a concerted effort to jointly grapple with the key issues and for­
midable challenges endemic to urban physical examination. To illustrate, urban planning
experts proffer a macroscopic perspective and furnish specific case studies, geographic
information scientists are entrusted with the task of data collection and processing,
data scientists deploy advanced analytical techniques to unearth latent patterns and
trends, and artificial intelligence specialists embark on the exploration of intelligent sol­
utions with the aim of augmenting the model’s predictive and decision-making capabili­
ties. Through the implementation of astute team management and communication

6.4. Deep user engagement and feedback
User engagement and feedback play a crucial role in the development and application
process for the knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal data model for urban physical
examination (Metzler et al. 2003). This intricate process mandates not only the forging
of a close collaborative alliance between technical personnel and urban physical examin­
ation practitioners but also the extensive assimilation of perspectives and suggestions
proffered by residents and other stakeholders. The profundity and expanse of user
engagement exert a direct and profound impact on the model’s practical utility and
user contentment.
    In the initial instance, direct user participation endows the model with invaluable
firsthand data and real-world application scenarios. Urban physical examination pro­
fessionals, drawing on their expertise and experience, can identify potential strengths
and weaknesses of the model in the analysis of urban physical examination. Residents,
as direct experiencers of urban life, furnish feedback that is instrumental in enabling
the model to more precisely capture and mirror the exigencies and predilections of
the populace. Other stakeholders, such as environmental organizations and business
enterprises, are likewise capable of contributing sagacious insights into the model
from their respective vantage points. This diverse feedback is indispensable for improv­
ing the model’s functionality and optimizing its interface. In return, the urban physical
examination knowledge services based on this model offer several benefits. For urban
planners, it provides a scientific basis for planning and helps develop strategies that
better meet the city’s needs. For city decision-makers, it offers comprehensive and accu­
rate data and knowledge support to enhance digital urban management. For the public, it
provides a clear picture of urban development, increasing their sense of participation and
identification with urban growth and environmental protection. Ultimately, this pro­
motes sustainable social development.
    In addition, to ensure the effective utilization of user feedback, the implementation of
perpetual and robust mechanisms for user engagement is of the utmost exigency, as this
serves as the linchpin for the model’s long-term and sustainable development. To galva­
nize active feedback and user participation in the iterative amelioration process of the
model, a repertoire of measures has been instituted, including regular user surveys, feed­
back systems for knowledge services, and public forums. Through such initiatives, users
are not relegated to the status of passive recipients of the model; rather, they are actively
enlisted as stakeholders in its evolutionary journey. This approach not only expedites the
continuous enhancement of the model’s capabilities but also engenders a sense of user
ownership and satisfaction, thereby augmenting the model’s effectiveness and societal
acclaim. A model that garners widespread acceptance and active utilization by its users
is more predisposed to catalyze the democratization and transparency of urban planning
and management, thereby amplifying its value and conferring greater benefits upon
society.

7. Conclusions
Facing the demands for integrating multi-source heterogeneous spatiotemporal data and
providing customized knowledge services in urban physical examination, this paper pro­
poses a knowledge graph-based spatiotemporal data model for urban physical examin­
ation. The model not only enriches the theoretical framework of spatiotemporal data
organization and management in urban physical examination but also clarifies the tran­
sition path from theoretical models to technical applications. It innovatively adopts a
three-layer architecture of ‘semantics-data-scenario’. From the perspectives of the
semantic layer, data layer, and scenario layer, it constructs an urban physical examination
knowledge system, manages the entire process of spatiotemporal data in urban physical
examination, and provides scenario-oriented spatiotemporal representation. First,
addressing the issue that current urban physical examination spatiotemporal data
models focus on new indicator design and conceptual discussions without fully consid­
ering the application needs of urban physical examination, the ‘semantics-data-scenario’
conceptual model is proposed. It integrates knowledge from the semantic layer and data
from the data layer based on application needs and presents them through the scenario
layer in an intuitive and interactive visual manner to provide customized knowledge ser­
vices to users. Second, to solve the problem of traditional UML diagrams that use
complex nesting and associations to represent and process complex relationships, result­
ing in high complexity and poor readability, the ‘semantics-data-scenario’ logical model
is proposed. It uses a logical model combining UML diagrams and hypergraph ontology,
representing higher-order relationships as hyperedges to simplify the model structure
and improve the accuracy of multidimensional relationship expression. Finally, taking
the greenway construction in Guangzhou as a case study, the model demonstrates an
innovative application mode of urban physical examination knowledge service.
    The model transforms the traditional urban physical examination approach, which
relies on experience and single indicators. It comprehensively organizes and expresses
urban data and knowledge through knowledge graph, analyzing and mining the intrinsic
connections between greenway construction and urban ecology, transportation, and
population at multiple levels (themes, connotations, knowledge points, and indicators)
and granularities (macro-level cities, meso-level regions, and micro-level streets). This
helps understand urban greenways from multiple dimensions. Additionally, the
model’s scenario-oriented spatiotemporal representation presents abstract knowledge
and data in intuitive forms such as greenway distribution maps and indicator analysis
charts. This clearly shows the current situation, issues, and development trends of green­
way construction, making it easier for urban planners, decision-makers, and the public to
understand and promoting multi-department collaboration and public participation.
    Subsequent research can delve into performance testing and exploration at three
levels: data, semantics, and application scenarios, with the aim of further enhancing
the model’s universality and practicality. Specifically, the research can focus on extracting
richer and more profound information and knowledge from data, thereby strengthening
the model’s analytical depth and intellectual content. Additionally, the scope of the
model’s application should be expanded to explore its potential role in key areas such
as urban disease prediction and environmental risk assessment. Furthermore, we will
38      S. WANG ET AL.


place emphasis on how to integrate this model with other technologies in smart cities to
construct more efficient and sustainable urban management strategies.
